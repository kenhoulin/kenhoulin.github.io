Cover Letter

Not all materials you submit in the application will be read. In descending order, people will read your CV and cover letter but not necessarily your research or teaching statement and writing sample. Thus, it's very important for you to write a good cover letter.

A cover letter is like a movie trailer, a brochure for a museum exhibition, or a product description. It needs to be short and effective. It needs to motivate people to act. It needs to persuade the committee that this application is different from many other applications. It is the opening statement in a court room, in which you tell a story about what you offer. You're presenting the evidence that you are the person who will solve their problems. Remember, your readers have a lot to read. Once they lose interest, they are unlikely to return to your file.

The point of a cover letter is not to show how unique or original you are as a person. You want to be something that people are familiar with. The best-selling products are not products people never heard of but very similar products with superior features. The new iPhone is pretty much the old iPhone. It just takes better photos and has a bigger battery. A lot of time, effort, and resources are at stake when hiring a new faculty. You want to let the committee see that you are a low-risk, high-reward investment.

Writing a good cover letter requires some double consciousness. You need to be able to look at yourself through the eyes of various search committees. You need to know how to translate what you know into something familiar and digestible to your readers. You need to think about what may draw them in. The language should be crisp, succinct, and free of jargon.

If this is too abstract, the demographers in this workshop should imagine how their letters will be read by the ethnographer and vice versa. How can you talk about yourself in a way that they think you will be a good colleague?

Yes, what a strong candidate looks like will vary by the type of institution and the hiring area. However, the iron law of sociology job market is that there are always more votes outside of your area than in your area. Of course, you want the people in your area to like you but they have a lot more information to judge whether you're a strong candidate. Those outside of your area depend more on the story you tell. It is only normal they want to vote for a candidate they can better understand/appreciate.

You will read a few examples and other participants' letters. Underline the sentences that are effective and those that are confusing. You should gain a sense of what a good letter looks like at the end.

Come back to this letter again and again, especially after you finish your research and teaching statements. It is likely you will have a better sense of what to write at that point.

Basic Structure

Cover letters usually follow a certain format/structure so the readers can go through them quickly. Unless you know the reason, I do not recommend that you deviate from the convention. The main components are:

  • Introduction
  • Research
  • Teaching
  • Service

The letter should be around 2-3 pages and no longer than 4. You will be making various claims about yourself and it is more important to show than tell. Do not just state that "I am a productive researcher" or "I am a good teacher." You need to substantiate these claims with "I have published two sole-authored articles, both in good journals" or "I received a 4.8 out of 5 evaluation for this course I taught." Be direct and specific. Let the evidence speaks.

Introduction

The introductory paragraph identifies what types of sociologist you are. The committee needs to know your current institution, career status (e.g., ABD, Postdoc, ... etc.), and areas of expertise. You can claim multiple areas of expertise but they must be supported by your publications and dissertation. If you have problem identifying your areas, consider which sections you are affiliated with at the ASA or what specialty journals you (wish to) publish in. All these parameters help the committee identify whether you're the type of sociologist they are looking for. They also give the committee a context to evaluate your progress and generate a set of check boxes. For example, Postdocs are expected to have a better publication records than ABDs. Ethnographers are expected to have a book project.

Research/Dissertation

If you're applying for a research position, around 70-80% of your letter should be about your research. Here you go beyond the areas and specify the themes of you research. It is also a place where you showcase your some of earlier publications and substantiate your earlier claims of expertise. Depending on your portfolio, you can talk about your dissertation right away or start with some pre-dissertation projects.

Either way, you have to know what your dissertation is about. If you are not sure yet, it is a good time to figure it out. Just remember that what you write in the letter should be what you know at this point, not what will be in your dissertation or book.

Be specific about what question(s) your research addresses. Do not describe your research as exploring X, Y, Z. People would just think that you don't know what your research is actually about.

Explain why your research is important. Why should anyone care about your research? We are highly segregated by research areas, and many students are not used to explain why their research is worthwhile. The universe is immense. Filling a gap is not a very strong reason to do research. Motivate the committee to care about what you do. Do not assume they will share the same view as you or the small group of people you know.

What does your research argue? Make a clear contrast between your arguments and existing arguments. Without doing so, your conclusion may seem obvious. Relatedly, how do we know that your arguments are better? Do you have better data? Is there any methodological innovation?

These are some existential questions you must keep in mind when writing about your research. The failure to answer any of them will heavily discount the value of your research. If you have done your proposal properly, you should already have the answers to some of these questions. They are easier to answer for some dissertations than for others. If you are still planning your dissertation, please take them into account. If you're in the middle of doing it, you need to think hard about these questions. In any case, remember that your letter will be read by people who are not familiar with the literature.

These questions are particularly important to answer when the work is not yet published. Having a clear description of your dissertation also gives the committee the confidence that you will indeed finish your dissertation on time and be ready for the new job.

All these principles apply when you're writing your research statement. The only difference is that you will have more space to go into the details. In the meantime, do not hide important things in the research statement. It is a supplementary material that some departments do not request.

Teaching

For research positions, you should have a couple of paragraphs about teaching. No department wants to hire a terrible teacher, no matter how great his research is. This is the place where you assure that their nightmare won't come true. It is often comforting to know that the candidate has taught some classes and received good evaluation from their students. Mention what you can teach, particularly those that are taught in most departments such as theory, statistics, and methods.

Remember, teaching does not only take place in classroom. If you have mentored or given workshops to undergraduate or graduate students, those activities may be worth mentioning. For teaching positions, you want to elaborate a bit more. See the readings about teaching and the examples for inspiration.

Service

An important part of a tenure-track position is service. You should have a couple of paragraphs describing the services you have provided at UT and to the discipline (e.g., ASA, PAA... etc.). It is necessary to show that you will be a good colleague that shoulder your share of service, not a selfish person who does not contribute to the community. What are the positions have you held? What activities have you volunteered for? This is the time you get some credit for your work.

For teaching and service, the evidence needs to be relevant to the academic setting. If the connection is not clear, you have to point out why the experience is relevant. For example, do not just say you have worked as a tutor. Talk about how this experience equipped you with the skills to work with disadvantaged students. These skills include X, Y, and Z. Similarly, talk about how the services you provided make you a better colleague.

ETC

  • Explain what the roles you play in your collaborative projects.
  • Write a generic letter for most applications. Unless the position is a perfect fit, do not extensively reivse your letter for each position.
