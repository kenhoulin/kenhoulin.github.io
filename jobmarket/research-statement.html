In the cover letter page, I have mentioned some basic questions you should address when describing your research project. Here I will provide some further discussion. Doing research is very different from writing about your research to a general audience. It is only normal if you don't know where to start.

To make this something more relatable, I want you to think about a very mundane decision you make regularly—which restaurants should I go to or order food from? If you often have a hard time making this decision, let me make it even harder: which restaurant should I go to regularly for the next 10-30 years?

Many of you will quickly narrow down your options to those that are highly rated on the internet. This is why having a competitive record is the most important thing. It will motivate the committee to pay more attention to what you have to say.

Living in Austin means that using rating to decide only solves 30% of the problem. There are so many highly-rated restaurants, what do you do next?

Research Identity

Maybe you will ask yourself what types of food you're in the mood to eat, just like a search committee may be searching for certain types of scholar to hire. Thus, in both the cover letter and research statement, it is important to declare a research identity.

A well-described identity serves two purposes. First, it gives a general sense of what type of food the restaurant serves. Is it French, Dinner, Ethiopian, Vegetarian, or French Fusion? Very rarely will you pick a restaurant you do not know what type of food it will serve. Similarly, the committee wants to know what type of scholar you will be and how they can understand you based what they already know.

It may appear advantageous to claim many areas of expertise. After all, you want to be considered by as many potential employers as possible. Yet, it is still important to have a coherent identity. Would you trust a restaurant that promises to serve both excellent enchilada and sashimi? I don't. Thus, you have to clearly identify what your core identity is. My recommendation is to describe your research agenda as clearly as possible. Listing no more than 3 to 4 areas and make clear how these areas are connected.

Second, a well-written identity should also convey what unique features you have that make you an outstanding or promising scholar in the area(s). What are the special and smart approaches you take that are different? Do you only use organic, locally-sourced ingredients? Is your meat never frozen? Is your chef trained in certain region of Spain? Take a look at the websites of your favorite restaurants. I expect all of them will tell you what they serve and what their unique features are.

To make it more concrete, let's take a look at the following paragraph from Sarah Brayne's cover letter:

My research program uses qualitative and quantitative methods to examine surveillance of individuals by bureaucratic organizations. I focus on the way in which organizations collect, analyze and deploy the information they gather and the implications of these practices for social inequality. My work lies at the intersection of research on social stratification and inequality, the criminal justice system, organizations and technological change. Although there is valuable theoretical work in the sociology of surveillance, there is a dearth of theoretically informed empirical research on the relationship between the criminal justice system, the rise of big data, and the social consequences of the intersection of the two forces . For example, how has the adoption of big data analytics facilitated new surveillance practices within criminal justice institutions? I address this gap by examining the way in which the adoption of data analytics is transforming surveillance practices, and the social consequences of such technological and organizational changes.

Another example from Maricarmen Hernandez's research statement:

My core research agenda is to examine how everyday experience and understandings of contamination contribute to the sociopolitical production of environmental inequality . To do so, I investigate how the processes of historical meaning making and frame building have environmental justice implications, as they work to obscure the mechanisms that trap communities into hazardous living spaces . My research links the literatures on social movements, contaminated communities and environmental justice, furthering our understanding of the toxicity and precarity that many marginalized communities in the global south face.

As you can see, both provide a very clear description of what type of food they serves and what the unique features are.

What if I have multiple research identities?

In this day and age it is not uncommon that graduate students may have multiple research areas and identities. Sometimes it could be fairly difficult to tell a coherent story. My recommendation is to prioritize what you do in your dissertation. A well-planned dissertation, even when consisting of three separate papers, should have some common themes. Only make claims about unrelated areas when you have publications about them. Still, leave them to a secondary place in your letter or statement. It is unwise to talk about everything at once.

Writing about your research

Now that a certain restaurant has caught your attention, you may want to take a look at the menu and see what specific dishes they serve. Menus are usually organized into different sections: appetizers, main dishes, and desserts. Similarly, you should figure out how to organize your research portfolio. If you have some publication records that are thematically related to your dissertation, you may organize your work into the following three sections:

  • Projects that are already published
  • Projects that are currently in progress/Dissertation
  • Future research projects.

If you don't, it is also okay to start with your dissertation. If your publications are in a total different area, discuss them after you finish your centerpiece.

While there is no law regarding how a menu should be written, it always provides you a basic sense about what ingredients are in each dish, including the type of vegetable or meat, spice, and how it is cooked. When writing about your research, consider each of the following question carefully:

  • What is the research question?

  • Why is it important for people to know the answer of this question?

  • What is the main argument?

    • How is it different from what other people argue?

  • What is the unique theoretical/methodological approach?

    • How is it different from how other people usually approach this issue?

  • What is the finding/conclusion? What are the implications?

I want to emphasize again that research topic research question. The former is too general for the committee to understand exactly what you do. A second thing to consider is contextualize your research—what is the current state of the literature and how you advance it? I know it sounds like a broken record at this point but you need to explain why your research is significant to people who are not familiar with this area of research.
