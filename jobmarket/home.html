Job Market Workshop

The purpose of this workshop is to help Sociology PhD students navigate the job market. It will run over the summer with maybe some follow-ups in the fall and winter. This workshop mainly focuses on academic jobs and we may organize a non-academic job workshop down the road.

A Collective Endeavor

Looking for a job is difficult and overwhelming in many ways but it will be easier if we do it together. The workshop is designed for students who plan to look for a job in the fall or in the following year. I welcome everyone to join the workshop but I do expect all participants to actively contribute to this experience.

This includes but does not limit to:

  • Complete all the required readings and writing before discussion
  • Attend the discussion sessions on a regular basis
  • Share your materials with others and future participants
  • Read other people's materials and provide honest and constructive feedback
  • Attend other people's practice job talks
  • Report to me when you receive an invitation or offer

Those who do not plan to look for a job this year are not required to submit any materials but are expected to comment on other people's materials and take note for the discussion sessions.

As much as I would like this workshop to provide some functions of a support group, the main emphasis is still on preparing you to be a competitive candidate on the job market. This means that all participants will have to recognize their strengths and confront their weaknesses as a job candidate in this process.

The summer workshop consists of 5 or 6 2-hour sessions. A note organizer will be assigned for each session. This person will take and collect notes from other people and send me a bullet-point write-up within a week after the session.

These sessions are organized around the materials you need when applying for an academic job. All active job seekers are expected to upload a draft 3 days before the session. All participants are expected to read and comment on these drafts.

I expect that you will revise these materials multiple times after receiving feedback from other participants and your advisors. Please make sure your writing is highly accessible. Specifically seek out opinions of those who are not familiar with your work and your areas of research. These people will give you the best feedback.

If you are interested in joining the workshop, please register using this Google form. As I do not know how much interest there is, I cannot guarantee that all the applicants will be admitted to the workshop. If you are not admitted, it is mostly because I don't think I am capable of helping you in this process. It says nothing about my opinions about you or your work.

The workshop will cover the following topics:

The Lay of the Land

We begin the workshop by mentally preparing ourselves for the job market. We will discuss questions such as:

  • Am I ready for the job market?
  • How to be competitive for certain types of jobs?
  • What are the career options for Sociology PhD students?
  • How are employers looking for?
  • What are the things I should do this summer?

We will also discuss where to find the jobs and how to read job ads. All active job seekers are expected to upload their CVs 3 days before the discussion. All participants are expected to upload two CVs of PhD students in a different top sociology program, preferably someone in your research areas.

Cover Letter

This is the document that tells a story about yourself in 2-3 pages. It is a investment proposal to motivate other people to invest in your career. Your goal is to persuade the readers that you are an ideal candidate for this position using a mix of show and tell. It has to be highly accessible to readers who are not familiar with your areas of research. We will focus on the following questions:

  • What is your scholar identity?

    • What are your substantive areas?
    • What is your methodological expertise (you know enough to teach a graduate seminar)?
    • What is rare about you as a scholar?

  • Why is your dissertation about?

    • Why is the topic of your dissertation important?
    • What are the main arguments of your dissertation?
    • How do you support these arguments?
    • Why is your dissertation innovative or unique in solving common problems?
    • How does your dissertation change existing understanding of the world?

  • What are the evidence showing that you will be a productive and impactful researcher?

  • What are the evidence showing that you will be an effective teacher?

  • What are the evidence showing that you will be a good citizen in the department?

These questions provide a basic outline for your cover letter. All active job seekers are expected to upload a draft of the cover letter 3 days before the discussion. All participants are expected to read them.

Research Statement

This document elaborates the research component of your cover letter. This does not mean that you should geek out and go into all the minutia. The main purpose of this document is to substantiate the claim that you will have a promising career as a researcher. You should still avoid using any jargon and make sure the document is accessible to any reader outside of your areas. You may consider using sections to organize the document if you have multiple projects. It still needs to be clear what your main research areas are. When writing about your research projects, consider the following questions:

  • What records do you have to show that you're an independent, productive researcher?
  • Why are your research topics important?
  • What are some smart ways you develop to solve the problems in the existing literature?
  • What are the intellectual or policy implications of your research?
  • What is your future research agenda?

Teaching Statement

This document illustrates why you will be an effective teacher. It would be difficult to write if you do not have any experience teaching your own courses. You should invest your time in this document if you are interested in working at a teaching institutions such as small liberal art colleges. You should provide very concrete evidence and example to support your claims. Consider the following questions:

  • What courses can you teach?

    • What are the experiences qualify you?

  • What courses have you taught?

  • How do you teach your courses differently or more innovatively?

  • What are the feedback you have received from your students?

  • What trainings or awards have you received about teaching?

  • How do you make your courses inclusive of disadvantaged students (e.g., minority, international, first-generation)?

Mock Interviews

Besides preparing for the materials, it is also important for you to adopt the identity as a job seeker. This means that you need to feel comfortable talking to strangers about yourself and your research. Please develop a 5-minute, 15-minute, and 30-minute pitch about yourself. Think about the answers to common interview questions during an ASA or phone/Zoom interview, such as:

  • Tell us about your dissertation.
  • What courses do you plan to teach?
  • What do you plan to do next?
  • What do you think about living in a rural area/big city?

I plan to invite a faculty at UT or some other institution to do 10 to 15-minute interview during the discussion session. For this session, please also put on a formal outfit.
